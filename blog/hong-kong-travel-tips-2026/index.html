Hong Kong Travel Tips 2026 - First Timer Guide

Your no-nonsense guide to visiting Hong Kong in 2026.

Hong Kong rewards travelers who know how to navigate it - and penalizes those who do not. It is a city where a 10-minute MTR ride and a HKD 15 egg waffle can sit alongside a HKD 800 hotel night and a HKD 639 Disneyland ticket. Knowing where the value is, and where it is not, makes a significant difference to the total cost of a Hong Kong trip. This guide covers everything a first-time visitor from Singapore, Malaysia, or the Philippines needs to know before arrival.

Before You Go - What to Prepare

Singapore and Malaysia passport holders receive 90 days visa-free entry to Hong Kong - no pre-arrangement required. Philippines passport holders need to check current entry requirements, as visa policies can change. Confirm with the Hong Kong Immigration Department website before booking flights.

Currency is Hong Kong Dollars (HKD). The exchange rate for Singapore travelers is roughly 1 SGD = 5.7 HKD. Credit cards are accepted almost universally in shops, restaurants, and hotels - more widely than in Thailand or Indonesia. Cash is still needed for markets, street food, and older cha chaan tengs.

Get an eSIM before you fly. Airport SIM card kiosks at HKIA charge significant premiums over pre-purchased plans. Airalo and Saily both offer Hong Kong data plans from around $3-5 for 1GB, which is far cheaper than airport rates. Our eSIM comparison guide has full pricing details for all providers.

The Octopus card is essential. It is a stored-value card that works on the MTR, buses, trams, ferries, and in convenience stores, most supermarkets, and many restaurants. Pick one up at the airport (HKD 150 including HKD 100 stored value and HKD 50 refundable deposit) and load it with HKD 200-300 for a 3-4 day trip.

Best time to visit: October to December is ideal - temperatures drop to a comfortable 18-25C, skies are clear, and visibility from the Peak is at its annual best. Avoid July and August: 35C heat, high humidity, and the peak of typhoon season. March to May is shoulder season with mild weather and lower hotel prices.

Getting Around Hong Kong

The MTR is Hong Kong's subway system and one of the best urban transit networks in the world. Clean, air-conditioned, punctual to within seconds, and covering all major areas of Kowloon and Hong Kong Island. Fares run HKD 4-55 depending on distance. With an Octopus card, you get a small discount per trip versus single-journey tickets.

The Star Ferry crossing between Tsim Sha Tsui and Central is one of the great travel experiences of Asia - HKD 3.40 upper deck, HKD 2.70 lower deck, and 8 minutes of harbor views that no rooftop bar can replicate. Take it at least once, ideally at dusk.

The Airport Express is the fastest link between HKIA and the city (24 minutes to Hong Kong Station, 21 minutes to Kowloon). Counter price is HKD 115 single / HKD 205 return. Pre-booking via Klook saves approximately 25-30% and delivers a QR code to your phone - no need to queue at the ticket machine after a long flight.

Trams (locally called "ding dings") run along the northern shore of Hong Kong Island for a flat HKD 3 fare. They are slow - do not use them when you are in a hurry - but they are charming and cheap, and the upper deck offers excellent street-level views of the shophouses and neon signs of Wan Chai and Causeway Bay.

Taxis in Hong Kong are metered and honest by Southeast Asian standards - red taxis cover urban areas, green taxis the New Territories, blue taxis Lantau Island. Starting fare is HKD 27. They are a legitimate option for late-night travel when the MTR is closed, but for most daytime journeys, the MTR is faster and cheaper.

Where to Stay

Tsim Sha Tsui (TST) on the Kowloon side is the classic first-timer choice - walking distance from the harbor, well-connected by MTR, surrounded by shopping and food options, and with mid-range hotels from around HKD 500-800 per night. The harbor view from the Tsim Sha Tsui promenade at night is one of the best free sights in the city.

Mong Kok is more local in character - a dense, busy neighborhood with night markets (Ladies Market, Temple Street), excellent cheap food, and budget hotels from HKD 300-500. It lacks TST's polish but more than compensates with authentic Hong Kong street life. Good choice if you are comfortable navigating a busier environment.

Central and Sheung Wan on Hong Kong Island are premium locations - walkable to many major sights, excellent dining and bar scenes, and easy MTR connections. Expect to pay HKD 700-1,200+ for hotels in this area. The convenience premium is real but so is the price.

Must-Do Experiences

Victoria Peak, accessed via the Peak Tram funicular, is the non-negotiable Hong Kong experience. The views of the harbor and city skyline are extraordinary. The queue for the tram can run 45-90 minutes during peak hours - pre-booking a Klook Peak Tram combo ticket skips the main queue and saves HKD 20-40 versus counter price.

Ngong Ping 360 on Lantau Island - the cable car ride to the Tian Tan (Big) Buddha - is one of the most scenic journeys in Hong Kong. The crystal cabin option (glass floor) is worth the upgrade. Book via Klook for better pricing and to guarantee the crystal cabin - they sell out fast on weekends. See our Klook Hong Kong promo codes for the latest discounts.

Hong Kong Disneyland is smaller than its counterparts in Tokyo and California but genuinely fun, particularly for families. Klook ticket prices (around HKD 550-580 for adults) are consistently 10-15% below the gate price of HKD 639. Ocean Park is a strong alternative with thrill rides, marine life exhibits, and shows - also bookable via Klook at a discount.

The Temple Street Night Market in Jordan is free to browse and worth an evening wander - fortune tellers, dim sum restaurants, street food, and bargain shopping. Negotiate on everything. A Macau day trip (1 hour by high-speed ferry from the Macau Ferry Terminal) is easily arranged via Trip.com or Klook with ferry tickets and optional Macau Tower bundled.

Food Guide - What and Where to Eat

Hong Kong's food scene ranges from some of the world's best Cantonese restaurants to HKD 15 egg waffles from a street cart. Budget well for food - it is one of the great pleasures of the city.

Dim sum is essential. Tim Ho Wan - started as a tiny bakery and became Michelin-starred - serves exceptional BBQ pork buns and rice noodle rolls at prices that seem impossible for the quality (HKD 30-50 per dish). Expect a queue but it moves fast. Lin Heung Tea House in Sheung Wan is the traditional alternative, a loud, chaotic, genuinely old-school dim sum hall where carts are pushed between packed tables.

Roast goose from Yat Lok in Central (Michelin Bib Gourmand) is among the finest roast poultry you will eat anywhere - HKD 60-80 per plate with rice. Queue before opening or expect a wait. The egg waffles (gai daan jai) from any of the street carts around Mong Kok are the definitive Hong Kong street snack at HKD 12-20.

For milk tea - the original Hong Kong style, pulled through a silk stocking strainer - Lan Fong Yuen in Central is the institution. A cup costs HKD 20-25. Cha chaan tengs (Hong Kong-style diners) serve complete breakfast sets (toast, egg, coffee or tea, instant noodles) for HKD 30-50. Eating local at these sets your daily food budget at HKD 150-250 (SGD 26-44) for three satisfying meals.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Not getting an Octopus card is the most common first-timer mistake. Single-journey MTR tickets cost more per ride, cannot be used on trams or buses without additional tickets, and involve queuing at machines every trip. The Octopus card costs HKD 150 at the airport and pays back the inconvenience cost immediately.

Visiting the Peak on a cloudy or hazy day is a common disappointment. Check the weather forecast the morning of your visit - if visibility is poor, rearrange your schedule and go on a clear day. The view is the entire point of the trip up.

Exchanging money at the airport is expensive. The Chungking Mansions in TST (ground floor money changers) offer some of the best exchange rates in Hong Kong - typically 3-5% better than airport counters. Withdraw HKD from a Hong Kong ATM using a card with zero FX fees as an alternative.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Hong Kong expensive for Singaporean travelers?
Mid-range to expensive compared to Bangkok or KL, but reasonable versus Tokyo or Seoul. Hotels are the biggest cost - expect HKD 500-900 (SGD 88-158) per night for a decent mid-range room. Food can be very cheap if you eat local. Attractions booked via Klook are significantly cheaper than walk-up prices. A well-planned 4-day trip including flights can be done for SGD 600-900 from Singapore.
How many days do I need in Hong Kong?
Three to four days covers the main highlights comfortably - Victoria Peak, Ngong Ping, a temple or two, the Star Ferry, dim sum, and a Macau day trip if you want it. Five to six days allows for a slower pace, more neighborhood exploration (Sham Shui Po, Sheung Wan), and a day trip to the outlying islands.
Is English widely spoken in Hong Kong?
Yes. English is an official language of Hong Kong and is widely spoken in hotels, restaurants, shops, and public services. MTR signage, menus, and official communications are all bilingual. You will have no difficulty navigating the city as an English speaker.
Should I book a Macau day trip in advance?
Yes, particularly for the ferry. TurboJET ferries between Hong Kong and Macau sell out on weekends and public holidays. Book at least 2-3 days ahead via Klook or Trip.com. The journey takes about 1 hour. A day in Macau - Senado Square, the Ruins of St Paul, egg tarts, and the Macau Tower - is a very worthwhile addition to a HK trip.
Is the Octopus card worth getting for a short trip?
Yes, even for 2-3 days. The card covers MTR, buses, trams, the Star Ferry, and purchases at 7-Eleven and most convenience stores. The HKD 50 deposit is fully refunded when you return the card. The convenience of tap-and-go versus buying individual tickets every trip is worth it from the first day.
